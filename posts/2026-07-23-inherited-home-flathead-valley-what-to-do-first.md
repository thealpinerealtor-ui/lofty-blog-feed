# Inherited a Home in the Flathead Valley? Start Here

If you've recently inherited a home in Kalispell, Whitefish, or anywhere in the Flathead Valley, here's the question I hear most often: "What am I actually supposed to do first?"

It's usually asked by someone who is grieving, coordinating with siblings in three different time zones, and staring at a house full of forty years of memories. And here's what most people get wrong: they assume everything is urgent. It isn't. A few things genuinely are — and knowing the difference is where families either protect the estate's value or quietly lose a piece of it.

Let's walk through it in plain English.

## First: understand what probate actually is (and isn't)

Probate is simply the court-supervised process of settling an estate — validating the will, appointing a personal representative, paying debts, and distributing what's left. In Montana, most estates go through what's called informal probate, which is far less dramatic than the word "probate" sounds.

Two things matter for the house specifically. One: in most cases, nobody can legally sell the property until the court appoints a personal representative (sometimes called an executor) with authority to act. Two: Montana probate typically runs a minimum of several months from start to finish, and often longer when real property is involved.

I'm a real estate agent, not an attorney, so treat this as orientation, not legal advice — a Montana probate attorney should confirm how the process applies to your specific estate. But the practical takeaway is this: the sale of the home usually happens *inside* a longer legal timeline, which means you have more breathing room than you think.

## What's actually urgent in the first 30 days

Not the sale. Not the price. Not the flooring. Here's what is:

**Secure and insure the property.** A vacant home in the Flathead Valley is exposed — frozen pipes in winter, unnoticed roof leaks, and the simple reality that empty houses attract problems. Call the insurance carrier and tell them the home is unoccupied; many homeowner policies restrict or lapse coverage on vacant properties, and an uncovered loss can take a six-figure bite out of the estate.

**Keep utilities on.** Heat in winter is non-negotiable here. A burst pipe in January can do more damage in a weekend than a bad sale price ever would.

**Locate the documents.** The will, the deed, any mortgage statements, property tax records, and insurance policies. The personal representative will need all of it.

**Do not start giving things away.** Until the personal representative is appointed and the estate's obligations are clear, the contents of the home are estate property. Well-meaning family members emptying the garage in week one creates conflict — and sometimes legal problems — later.

## What can wait (and usually should)

Everything cosmetic. Families under stress often pour money into renovations an inherited home doesn't need, or swing the other way and dump the property as-is at a discount because "we just want this done."

Both cost real money. The right move is a clear-eyed assessment: what does this specific home, in this specific neighborhood, actually need to sell well? Sometimes that's a deep clean, a dumpster, and fresh landscaping. Rarely is it a $60,000 kitchen. An agent who knows the Kalispell and Whitefish markets can walk the property and tell you where the line is — before you spend a dollar.

The same goes for clearing out the home. It has to happen, but it doesn't have to happen this month. Estate sale companies, donation pickups, and family distribution all work better with a plan than with a panic.

## Where families lose money by rushing

After years of working with estates and other high-stakes sales, the pattern is consistent. Families lose money in three places:

**Selling before the market can respond.** Accepting an early, unsolicited cash offer because it feels simple. Those offers exist because they're profitable — for the buyer. A properly prepared and marketed home in the Flathead Valley almost always nets the estate meaningfully more, even after the extra weeks.

**Skipping the tax conversation.** Inherited property generally receives a step-up in basis — meaning capital gains are typically calculated from the value at the date of death, not what your parents paid in 1987. That can dramatically reduce the tax owed on a sale. But the details matter and situations vary, so before you sell, talk to a CPA. This is orientation, not tax advice.

**Letting distance drive the decision.** Many heirs I work with live out of state. Managing showings, contractors, and paperwork from Denver or Seattle feels impossible, so they take the path of least resistance. It doesn't have to work that way. With the right local team — agent, attorney, cleanout crew — an out-of-state personal representative can run the entire sale remotely with weekly updates and zero surprises.

An estate sale is a mission with phases — secure the asset, establish legal authority, prepare, market, close. Handled in order, it's manageable. Handled in a panic, it's expensive.
